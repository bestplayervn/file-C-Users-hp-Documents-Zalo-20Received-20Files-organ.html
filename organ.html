Human Anatomy

Major Organs and their Functions

The Heart and Lungs

The heart, located within the chest cavity, is cone-shaped and has four chambers: two upper atria and two lower ventricles.

Coronary arteries and veins nourish the heart muscle. Valves ensure blood flows in one direction only, between atria and ventricles, and ventricles and arteries.

Surrounding the lungs are two pleural membranes. The outer layer (parietal pleura) sticks to the chest wall, and the inner layer (visceral pleura) covers the lungs. Between them is a thin layer of fluid that allows the lungs to expand.

Lungs contain millions of tiny air sacs called alveoli, providing a large surface area for gas exchange between inhaled air and blood.

The Digestive System

Stomach

The stomach is a pouch-like organ that stores and breaks down food. It has four main layers: a muscular outer layer, a submucosa layer, and a mucosa layer containing gastric glands that produce digestive juices.

Small Intestine

The small intestine is a long, narrow tube responsible for absorbing nutrients from food. It also has four layers similar to the stomach and contains villi, finger-like projections that increase the surface area for absorption.

Large Intestine

The large intestine absorbs water and electrolytes from digested food waste and forms feces for elimination.

Other Organs

Liver

The liver, with four lobes, is responsible for various functions like filtering blood, producing bile, and storing nutrients.

Uterus (Female Reproductive System)

The uterus is a pear-shaped organ in females involved in menstruation, pregnancy, and childbirth.

Kidneys

Each person has two kidneys, which filter waste products from the blood, maintain fluid balance, and regulate blood pressure.

Brain

The brain, the body's control center, is divided into three main parts: cerebrum, brainstem, and cerebellum. It is composed of neurons and glial cells and is responsible for various functions like thought, movement, and sensation.
